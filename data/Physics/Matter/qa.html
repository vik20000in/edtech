Physics: Matter - Questions and Answers

Q: What is matter?

Matter is defined as anything which occupies space and has mass[cite: 1, 38]. It can be perceived by our senses[cite: 39].

Q: What are the three states of matter?

The three states of matter are solid, liquid, and gas[cite: 2, 41].

Q: What is matter composed of?

Matter is composed of a large number of molecules[cite: 2], which are tiny particles known as atoms[cite: 40].

Q: What is a molecule?

A molecule is the smallest particle (or unit) of matter which can exist freely/independently in nature by itself and retains the properties of the substance[cite: 3, 45].

Q: What is the approximate size of a molecule?

A molecule is very small in size, approximately 10-10 m or 10-9 m[cite: 5, 47].

Q: What is inter-molecular spacing?

The space between any two consecutive molecules of a substance is called inter-molecular spacing (or space)[cite: 5, 50].

Q: What are inter-molecular forces?

The forces acting between the molecules which hold them together in a substance are called the inter-molecular forces[cite: 6]. It is the force of attraction between molecules[cite: 55].

Q: What are the forces of cohesion and adhesion?

The force of attraction between the molecules of the same substance (similar kind) is called the force of cohesion[cite: 7, 57]. The force of attraction between the molecules of two different substances (different types) is called the force of adhesion[cite: 7, 59].

Q: State three characteristics of molecules of matter.

Characteristics of molecules: 1. They are very small in size[cite: 61]. 2. They have spaces between them[cite: 62]. 3. They are in constant random motion[cite: 62]. 4. They always attract each other[cite: 62].

Q: How do molecules move in solids, liquids, and gases?

In solids, molecules vibrate to and fro about their mean positions but do not leave their positions[cite: 11, 12, 85]. In liquids, molecules are free to move within the boundary of the liquid[cite: 13]. In gases, molecules are free to move anywhere in space[cite: 14]. The molecular motion in liquid and gas follows a zig-zag path[cite: 25, 68].

Q: Why does a solid have a definite shape and volume?

In a solid, molecules are very tightly packed with high inter-molecular force[cite: 84]. The molecules do not move from their mean position, only vibrate, thus solids have a definite shape and volume[cite: 85].

Q: Why does a liquid have a definite volume but no definite shape?

In liquids, molecules are less tightly packed than solids with weaker inter-molecular forces[cite: 88, 96]. Molecules can move within the liquid[cite: 88]. Thus, they acquire the shape of the vessel but have a definite volume at a given temperature[cite: 89, 98].

Q: Why does a gas have neither a definite shape nor a definite volume?

In gases, molecules are far apart with the weakest inter-molecular forces[cite: 91, 99]. Molecules are not bound and move about freely, so gases have neither a definite shape nor a definite volume[cite: 92, 100].

Q: What is meant by the change of state of matter?

The change in state of matter of a substance, such as from solid to liquid or liquid to gas, brought about by imparting heat energy at a constant temperature[cite: 104].

Q: What is melting (or fusion)?

It is the process of change of a substance from solid state into its liquid state on absorption of heat at a particular constant temperature, called the melting point[cite: 105].

Q: What is boiling (or vaporization)?

It is the process of change of a substance from a liquid state to its gaseous state at a particular constant temperature, called the boiling point[cite: 106].
