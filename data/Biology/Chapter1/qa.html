Q: Differentiate between the following: (i) Tap root and Fibrous root

Tap root:
1. This root has one main primary root with many side secondary roots[cite: 12].
2. It is found in dicot plants[cite: 12].
3. e.g. mango, pea[cite: 13].
Fibrous root:
1. These roots are clusters of same thickness and size, arising from the base of the stem[cite: 13].
2. It is found in monocot plants[cite: 14].
3. e.g. maize, wheat[cite: 14].

Q: Differentiate between the following: (ii) Simple Leaf and compound leaf

Simple Leaf:
1. The Lamina is uni divided and is a single piece[cite: 14].
2. Example: mango, banana, banyan, etc[cite: 15].
Compound Leaf:
1. The leaf blade or lamina is divided into smaller units called leaflets[cite: 15].
2. Example is rose[cite: 16, 51].

Q: Differentiate between the following: (iii) Parallel venation and reticulate venation

Parallel Venation:
1. In this type of venation, veins run parallel to each other[cite: 18, 30, 53].
2. Examples are banana, grass and wheat leaves[cite: 18, 30].
Reticulate Venation:
1. In this type of venation, veins and veinlets are irregularly distributed in the lamina, forming a network[cite: 16, 29, 54].
2. Examples are peepal, mango and guava leaves[cite: 17, 30, 55].

Q: What are the four functions of the roots?

The root serves the following functions:
1. It fixes the plant in the soil[cite: 19].
2. Absorbs water and minerals from the soil for the entire plant[cite: 20].
3. It acts as a storage part for food materials for certain plants[cite: 21].
4. It binds the soil together so that it does not get washed away during rain or blown over by the wind[cite: 22].

Q: Mention the functions of the following: (i) Spines (ii) Tendril (iii) Scale leaves

(i) Spines: The leaves may be modified to form spines to reduce water loss by transpiration in desert plants (like cactus) or bear spines on the margin (like prickly poppy)[cite: 23, 46, 67].
(ii) Tendril: The stem or leaf/leaflet may occur as a thread-like, leafless, wiry, coiled structure called a tendril. It is sensitive to touch and coils around objects to help the plant climb[cite: 24, 25, 65, 66, 72]. E.g., Sweet pea[cite: 67, 73].
(iii) Scale leaves: Scale leaves are present in some plants like onion and ginger. They can be thin and dry or thick and fleshy, and their function is to protect buds[cite: 25, 26, 68, 69].

Q: Define venation. What are the different types of ve-nation found in the leaves?

Venation: Arrangement or pattern of veins in a lamina is called venation[cite: 28].
It is mainly of two types[cite: 29]:
1. Reticulate venation: Veins and veinlets are irregularly distributed in the lamina forming a network[cite: 29]. Example: mango, guava[cite: 30].
2. Parallel venation: Veins run parallel to each other[cite: 30]. Example: Banana, grass, wheat[cite: 30].

Q: Describe the modifications of leaf in any one insectivorous plant.

Modification of leaves in Venus flytrap (an insectivorous plant):
The leaves of Venus flytrap have long pointed hair[cite: 31]. It is divided into two parts with a midrib in between like a hinge[cite: 32]. When an insect visits the leaf, it closes its two parts and traps the insect[cite: 33]. The insect is then digested by secreting digestive juices[cite: 34].

Q: Write the two main functions of leaves.

The two main functions of leaves are:
1. Photosynthesis: Green leaves contain chlorophyll which, in the presence of sunlight, manufactures food using carbon dioxide and water[cite: 35, 48].
2. Transpiration: The surface of leaves has minute pores which help in the loss of water by evaporation[cite: 36, 48]. It has a cooling effect and causes suction, making roots absorb more water[cite: 37].

Q: Define: (i) Photosynthesis (ii) Transpiration

(i) Photosynthesis: The process by which a plant leaf prepares or synthesises food from water and carbon dioxide in the presence of chlorophyll and sunlight is called photosynthesis[cite: 41].
(ii) Transpiration: This is the process by which there is a loss of water in the form of vapour by evaporation from the surface of leaves[cite: 42]. It has a cooling effect and causes a suction force to make roots absorb more water with mineral ions[cite: 43].

Q: In list some of the advantages of transpiration to green plants.

Advantages of transpiration:
1. Maintains Sap Concentration: Helps maintain the concentration of sap by allowing continuous absorption of water and minerals as excess water evaporates[cite: 57, 58].
2. Cooling Effect: Evaporation of water cools the plant, especially when it's hot outside[cite: 59, 60].

Q: Why do some plants have to trap insects?

Insectivorous plants trap insects because they typically grow in soil deficient in nitrogen, and the insects help fulfill the plant's nitrogen requirement[cite: 62].

Q: Explain some of the modifications of leaves found in plants.

Some leaf modifications include:
1. Leaf tendril: In weak-stemmed plants like sweet pea, leaves or leaflets modify into wiry, coiled tendrils that help the plant climb by coiling around objects[cite: 65, 66, 67].
2. Spines: Leaves modify into spines (e.g., cactus, prickly poppy margin) to reduce water loss[cite: 67, 68].
3. Scale leaves: Found in plants like onion and ginger, these can be thin/dry or thick/fleshy and protect buds[cite: 68, 69].

Q: What is a tendril? Explain its use to the plant.

A tendril is a specialized stem, leaf, or petiole with a thread-like shape[cite: 71]. They are sensitive to touch and coil around objects they contact, supporting the plant and helping it to climb[cite: 72]. Example: Sweet pea[cite: 73].
